üß™ Try These Eye-Opening Experiments

Now let's see ChatGPT's limitations in action. Try these prompts yourself:

Experiment 1: Test Current Information

"What happened at [Your School Name] yesterday?"

What you'll discover: ChatGPT has no access to current, local, or personal information. It can't know about your school's specific events.

Experiment 2: Test Factual Accuracy

"List all the books written by [obscure author] with their publication dates"

What you'll discover: ChatGPT often "hallucinates" plausible-sounding but completely fictional information, especially for lesser-known topics.

Experiment 3: Test Understanding

"If all bloops are bleeps, and some bleeps are blops, are all bloops blops?"

What you'll discover: ChatGPT processes patterns, not meaning. It may give a confident answer to nonsensical logic problems.

Experiment 4: Test Consistency

Ask the same opinion question multiple times in new conversations, like: "What's the best way to teach fractions?"

What you'll discover: ChatGPT gives different answers each time because it doesn't have consistent opinions or beliefs.

üìö Myths vs. Reality

Common Myths

  • ChatGPT is connected to the internet
  • It understands what it's saying
  • It's always accurate
  • It can replace human thinking
  • Detection software works reliably
  • It learns from conversations
  • It has opinions and beliefs
  • Students only use it to cheat

The Reality

  • Works from training data with a cutoff date
  • Pattern matching, not comprehension
  • Often makes convincing errors
  • Tool to enhance human thinking
  • Detection is unreliable (50-60% accuracy)
  • Each conversation starts fresh
  • Generates probable responses
  • 78% want to use it "properly"

üí° What This Means for Your Classroom

If you thought ChatGPT was all-knowing...

You might have created policies based on fear rather than understanding. Many teachers ban AI because they think it can do students' thinking for them.

But knowing its limitations...

You can design assignments that ChatGPT can't complete alone, teach students to fact-check AI output, and use AI as a starting point for critical thinking rather than an endpoint.

Key Takeaways for Teachers:

  1. AI can't access personal or current information - Design assignments requiring local knowledge
  2. AI makes mistakes confidently - Teach students to verify everything
  3. AI doesn't understand meaning - Focus on comprehension and analysis
  4. AI is a pattern machine - Use it to show patterns, then go deeper
  5. Detection doesn't work reliably - Focus on process, not policing
